Petroleum supply · State of Alaska

Eight hundred miles of pipe — and most of it leaves the state.

North Slope crude rides the Trans‑Alaska Pipeline 800 miles south to Valdez, where the great majority is loaded onto tankers and shipped out. What stays is topped into jet and diesel; Anchorage runs on Cook Inlet gas, and the bush burns barged‑in diesel.

Alaska pumps far more oil than it burns — the crude rides 800 miles south to Valdez and sails away, while remote villages pay some of the highest fuel prices in America.

Reading the map. Ribbon width & particle density scale with annual volume (√ scaling). The fat blue spine is TAPS carrying North Slope crude to Valdez; the dashed blue leg is tanker export. Orange is refined product; teal is Cook Inlet gas to Southcentral power and the (mostly idle) Kenai LNG terminal. Hover any node for detail; toggle Vulnerability for supply‑fragility rings. Teal gas isn't just for power — the refineries themselves burn natural gas for process heat and hydrogen. Anchorage's outsized jet share reflects Ted Stevens (ANC), the #1 cargo airport in the U.S. and #3 in the world (ACI, 2025).
